The Romantic Period


1785-1832

History

The Romantic period, though by far the shortest, is at least as complex and diverse as any other period in British literary history, and it is, tellingly, demarcated differently than any of the other eras that literary historians and anthologists include in their timelines. (beginning with turbulence and ending with reformation)

  • 1787:Establishment of the Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Trade in Slaves
  • 1789-1815:Revolutionary and Napoleonic period in France.
  • 1807:British slave trade outlawed
  • 1811-20:The Regency--George, Prince of Wales, acts as regent for George III, who has been declareed incurably insane
  • 1819:Peterloo Massacre
  • 1820:Accession of George IV
  • 1830:Accession of William IV
  • 1832:Passage of the Reform Bill in Parliament

The New Poetries

Writers working in this period, from 1785 to 1832, did not think of themselves as constituting a group of "Romantic" authors. It was Victorian critics who first wrote of the previous generation as the Romantics and promoted the term as a description for a period of recent, modern rather than premodern, history. Comtemporaries, by contrast, treated these writers as independent individuals or else grouped them (usually maliciously, but with some basis in fact) into a number of separate "schools" or "sects":

Lake School
Cockney School
Satanic School

Lake School

The Lake Poets were a group of English poets who all lived in the Lake District of England, United Kingdom, in the first half of the nineteenth century. The Lake Poets were part of the Romantic Movement and are best remembered for verses related to natural imagery. Despite this, they did not follow a single idea or school of thought.

William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth

born on April 7, 1770, Cockermouth, Cumberland, England.
died on April 23, 1850, Rydal Mount, Westmorland.

He is the English poet whose Lyrical Ballads (1798), written with Samuel Taylor Coleridge, helped launch the English Romantic movement.

His most famous work is "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud"

Samuel Taylor Coleridge

born on October 21, 1772, Ottery St. Mary, Devonshire, England.
died on July 25, 1834, Highgate, near London.

English lyrical poet, critic, and philosopher.

His Biographia Literaria (1817) is the most significant work of general literary criticism produced in the English Romantic period.

Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Cockney School

A derogatory term for vulgar Londoners Leigh Hunt, William Hazlitt, and associated writers who had pretensions beyond their station, including Keat. The term was first used in a scathing review in Blackwood’s Magazine in October 1817, in which the anonymous reviewer mocked the poets' lack of pedigree and sophistication.

John Keats

John Keats

born on October 31, 1795, London, England.
died on February 23, 1821, Rome, Papal States(Italy).

English Romantic lyric poet who devoted his short life to the perfection of a poetry marked by vivid imagery, great sensuous appeal, and an attempt to express a philosophy through classical legend.

His most famous poem is " Ode to a Nightingale"

Satanic School

In reference to certain English poets whose works are believed to be “characterised by a Satanic spirit of pride and audacious impiety.” Lord Byron is the head of the Satanic school, and Byron’s friend Percy Bysshe Shelley is also generally understood to have belonged to Satanic school.

Lord Byron, in full George Gordon Byron, 6th Baron Byron

born on January 22, 1788, London, England.
died on April 19, 1824, Missolonghi, Greece.

British Romantic poet and satirist whose poetry and personality captured the imagination of Europe.

Renowned as the “gloomy egoist” of his autobiographical poem Childe Harold’s Pilgrimage (1812–18) in the 19th century, he is now more generally esteemed for the satiric realism of Don Juan (1819–24).

Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley

born on Aug. 4, 1792, Field Place, near Horsham, Sussex, England.
died on July 8, 1822, at sea off Livorno, Tuscany(Italy).

English Romantic poet whose passionate search for personal love and social justice was gradually channeled from overt actions into poems that rank with the greatest in the English language.

His most famous poem is "Ozymandias"
